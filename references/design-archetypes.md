# Design Archetypes

When a user lacks strong brand guidelines, present 2-3 of these archetypes as design direction options. Choose the most appropriate ones based on the product type and target audience.

---

## Clean Minimal

**Visual feel:** Generous whitespace, restrained palette, elegant typography, subtle interactions. Confidence through simplicity.

**Best for:** SaaS products, developer tools, premium products, productivity apps, design tools, high-end e-commerce.

**Key characteristics:**
- Monochromatic or limited color palette (1-2 accent colors max)
- Sans-serif typography with clean geometric forms (Inter, SF Pro, Geist)
- Ample whitespace — content breathes
- Subtle shadows and borders
- Minimal decoration — every element earns its place
- Micro-interactions are refined and understated

**Audit categories most improved:** Spacing & Rhythm, Overall Polish, Typography

**Tradeoffs:** Can feel cold or sterile if overdone. Requires excellent content to fill the space intentionally.

---

## Bold & Vibrant

**Visual feel:** Strong colors, expressive typography, dynamic layouts, energetic motion. Personality-forward and memorable.

**Best for:** Consumer apps, creative agencies, startups targeting younger audiences, entertainment products, social platforms.

**Key characteristics:**
- Saturated, high-contrast color palette
- Display typefaces with personality (plus clean body font)
- Asymmetric or unconventional layouts
- Bold gradients and color blocks
- Expressive animations and micro-interactions
- Strong visual personality in every component

**Audit categories most improved:** Branding & Identity, Visual Hierarchy, Motion & Interaction

**Tradeoffs:** Requires more animation work. Can feel overwhelming if not balanced. May alienate conservative audiences.

---

## Corporate Premium

**Visual feel:** Sophisticated palette, authoritative typography, structured layouts, polished but restrained. Trust and professionalism.

**Best for:** Enterprise SaaS, financial products, B2B platforms, legal/consulting services, healthcare.

**Key characteristics:**
- Muted, professional color palette (navy, slate, sophisticated grays)
- Serif headings for authority, clean sans-serif body
- Highly structured grid-based layouts
- Conservative use of color — mostly neutrals with strategic accent
- Subtle, professional motion (no flashy animations)
- Clear information hierarchy

**Audit categories most improved:** Layout & Structure, Accessibility, CTA & Conversion

**Tradeoffs:** Can feel generic if not executed with strong typography. Needs distinctive brand elements to avoid "template" feel.

---

## Startup Fresh

**Visual feel:** Friendly colors, approachable type, clean components, light and modern. Optimistic and accessible.

**Best for:** Early-stage products, productivity tools, community platforms, marketplaces, educational products.

**Key characteristics:**
- Bright but not overwhelming accent colors
- Rounded elements (buttons, cards, avatars)
- Friendly sans-serif typography (Plus Jakarta Sans, Nunito, Poppins)
- Illustrations or icons as visual support
- Light backgrounds with colorful accents
- Smooth, bouncy micro-interactions

**Audit categories most improved:** Component Design, Overall Polish, Branding & Identity

**Tradeoffs:** Can feel too "startup-y" or trendy. Rounded corners everywhere can undermine seriousness if overdone.

---

## Editorial & Content-First

**Visual feel:** Magazine-inspired layouts, strong typography focus, reading experience prioritized. Sophisticated and intellectual.

**Best for:** Blogs, publications, content platforms, documentation sites, portfolios, news sites.

**Key characteristics:**
- Serif typography for body text (excellent readability)
- Strong typographic hierarchy with dramatic size contrasts
- Column-based layouts inspired by print design
- Generous line-height and comfortable measure (55-70ch)
- Minimal UI chrome — content is the interface
- Subtle use of color to highlight without distracting

**Audit categories most improved:** Typography, Layout & Structure, Visual Hierarchy

**Tradeoffs:** Requires excellent content. Doesn't work well for data-heavy or interactive interfaces.

---

## Dark & Technical

**Visual feel:** Dark palette, monospace accents, data-dense layouts, precise. Serious and sophisticated.

**Best for:** Developer tools, analytics dashboards, technical products, trading platforms, gaming, creative tools.

**Key characteristics:**
- Dark backgrounds (slate, charcoal, near-black)
- High-contrast accent colors that pop on dark
- Monospace fonts for data, code, numbers
- Data visualization as a design element
- Compact, information-dense layouts
- Subtle borders and separators

**Audit categories most improved:** Branding & Identity, Component Design, Overall Polish

**Tradeoffs:** Requires careful attention to accessibility (contrast). Dark mode can be fatiguing for long reading sessions.

---

## How to Present Archetypes

When presenting options to the user, include for each archetype:

1. **2-sentence visual description** — what it will feel like
2. **Why it fits** — connection to their product type and audience
3. **Which audit categories** it will most improve
4. **Key tradeoffs** — honest assessment of limitations

Example:

> **Option A: Clean Minimal**
> Your product is a developer tool, and your audience values clarity over flashiness. Clean Minimal will dramatically improve your spacing and polish scores while maintaining the "serious tool" feel. The tradeoff is that you'll need strong content to fill the whitespace intentionally — empty space should feel purposeful, not neglected.
